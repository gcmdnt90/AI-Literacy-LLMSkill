# AI Literacy Standards Map

Use this map to design the course and interpret learner evidence. Do not turn it into a rigid checklist. The course should adapt to the learner's goals and demonstrated understanding.

## Primary References

- Stanford Teaching Commons, Artificial Intelligence Teaching Guide.

- MAILS: Meta AI Literacy Scale.
- MAILS short version.
- GAAIS: General Attitudes towards Artificial Intelligence Scale.

## Stanford AI Literacy Domains

Stanford organizes generative AI literacy into four intersecting domains under a human-centered approach:

- Functional literacy: how AI works, how to access and operate tools, what current tools can and cannot do.
- Ethical literacy: privacy, bias, equity, reliability, authorship, sustainability, integrity, and collective responsibility.
- Rhetorical literacy: how language, prompting, genre, audience, tone, voice, and AI-generated text shape outcomes.
- Pedagogical literacy: how AI can support or undermine learning, teaching, feedback, critical thinking, well-being, inclusion, and evidence-based practice.

Use these as the learner-facing curriculum domains when appropriate because they are intuitive and pedagogically useful.

## MAILS Dimensions

Use MAILS mainly for self-perceived AI literacy and confidence calibration:

- Use and apply AI.
- Know and understand AI.
- Detect AI.
- AI ethics.
- Create AI as a related but separable construct.
- AI self-efficacy and self-management as psychological meta-competencies.

Do not copy copyrighted questionnaire items into lessons unless the user provides licensed text. Create original diagnostic prompts aligned with these constructs.

## GAAIS and GAAIS-IT Attitude Signals

Use GAAIS/GAAIS-IT as an attitude lens, not as a full psychometric instrument unless the user explicitly asks for formal scale use.

Track two broad signals:

- Positive attitudes: perceived usefulness, opportunity, curiosity, enthusiasm, willingness to experiment.
- Negative attitudes: concern, distrust, discomfort, fear, perceived threat, privacy worry, job-risk worry.

Use these signals to personalize pacing and examples. High enthusiasm may need verification discipline. High concern may need safe, low-stakes practice and clearer boundaries.

## Generative AI Literacy: Twelve Competencies

Use this as the generative-AI-specific backbone. The exact phrasing of competencies varies across summaries and versions, so verify against the paper when precision matters.

1. Basic AI literacy.
2. Knowledge of generative AI models.
3. Knowledge of the capacities and limitations of generative AI tools.
4. Skill to use generative AI tools.
5. Ability to detect AI-generated content.
6. Ability to assess outputs of generative AI tools.
7. Prompt engineering.
8. Programming skills or technical integration skills where relevant.
9. Knowledge of context and domain fit.
10. Knowledge of ethical aspects.
11. Knowledge of legal aspects.
12. Continuous self-learning.

For nontechnical learners, treat programming and technical integration as optional or domain-specific rather than mandatory.

## Suggested Course Progression

Adapt this path to the learner:

1. Orientation and goals: what the learner wants to do with AI, current use, constraints, risks.
2. Functional foundations: LLMs, training, context windows, stochastic generation, retrieval, tool use, model/tool differences.
3. Practical interaction: prompting, iteration, task decomposition, tool selection, workflow design.
4. Critical evaluation: hallucinations, citations, uncertainty, verification, source triangulation, benchmark skepticism.
5. Data and risk: privacy, sensitive data, cloud/local tradeoffs, organizational policy, legal and ethical boundaries.
6. Rhetorical control: audience, genre, style, voice, collaboration boundary, attribution and authorship.
7. Domain transfer: apply the above to the learner's work, study, teaching, research, coding, or creative practice.
8. Advanced tracks: agents, RAG, automation, coding, multimodal generation, governance, evaluation, or custom tool building.

## Evidence Examples

Good evidence:

- The learner explains why an LLM may answer differently to the same prompt.
- The learner checks a citation and identifies unsupported claims.
- The learner distinguishes model memory, retrieval, context, and live search.
- The learner chooses a privacy-preserving workflow for sensitive data.
- The learner rewrites a prompt based on audience, genre, and constraints.
- The learner transfers a concept to a real task or teaches it to someone else.

Weak evidence:

- The learner says they "understand AI" without applying the concept.
- The learner relies on a tool's confidence or fluency as proof.
- The learner treats all AI risk as either negligible or catastrophic.
- The learner memorizes terminology but cannot diagnose failure modes.
